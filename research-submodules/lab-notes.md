We talk a lot about mammals. When measured by biomass (the total weight/mass of the animals, calculated in tons of carbon), the breakdown of mammals on Earth is:

60% Livestock (mostly cattle and pigs, raised for meat and dairy)
36% Humans
4% Wild mammals (everything from mice to elephants to whales)  

The exact statistics come from a landmark 2018 study published in the peer-reviewed journal PNAS (Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences). 

When looking at birds, 70% of all bird biomass on Earth is farmed poultry (like chickens), and only 30% are wild birds. 

On average, there has been a large decline across tens of thousands of wildlife populations since 1970

Humanity has caused the loss of 83% of all wild mammals.

The Mass: Before human civilization, global plant mass was estimated to be around 900 gigatons of carbon.
Today, due to massive deforestation (like cutting down the Amazon or old-growth forests to make room for livestock pasture and agriculture), the total weight of plants on Earth is around 450 gigatons. Humans have literally chopped down half the planet's plant weight.
